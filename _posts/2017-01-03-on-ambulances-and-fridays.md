## Newton's laws will always bring you down to Earth

When studying to become an EMT, you receive a massive book filled with the detailed descriptions of symptoms for both minor scrapes and sicknesses and near fatal traumas or illnesses. The first law of emergency services states: ninety-nine percent of your calls will be distributed between these two extremes. The second law of emergency services states: Ninety-nine percent of these calls will be glorified home checkup services, delivered with lights, sirens, and police.

On Friday afternoons, whenever the call was severe enough, the medic, Mark, would ride along with us. Most medics can't be bothered with an EMT trainee riding with them in the back of the bus. This is not necessarily arrogance or ill will. Nobody said a good medic needs to be a good teacher; they just need to keep patients in as stabilized a condition as possible until they can hand over care to the ER staff. Mark was different. In most of the calls, he would make the trainee take the patients' vital signs as long as they were capable of handling the pressure. Once, when the EMT crew chief second-guessed a particularly touch and go case, he was emphatic: "In a few months, they're going to be on their own. If they can't handle it with us as backup, then what happens when they are solo?”

On the ride back to the station, all our patient interaction was thrown back to us as clinical analysis. "When we arrived on scene, what was the patient initially presenting with, and what was your initial impression from their presentation?" Answer correctly, and you would get a sort of satisfied smirk, but answer wrong and you would get the “Mark face” (best described as a duck face with a furrowed brow). What he wanted to see from EMT students was their ability to apply the classroom knowledge to the real world. Our answers were scrutinized, and more important than any sort of facial expression of approval or disapproval, we knew that our progression as professionals was communicated up the chain of command to the EMS Corps lieutenants that ran the organization. 

All of this was in service to a greater purpose. Mark made us take what had been an exercise in memorizing facts for state exams and start applying that knowledge to real-world situations we were likely to encounter in the field. Because reality is less clear-cut than symptoms in a textbook, the exercise forced us to leave to leave the safety of predefined scenarios. We had to take information that gave us, at best, an incomplete picture of our situation and apply it as best as we could to the framework of emergency medicine. 


Not every case was a textbook presentation, and sometimes textbook presentations can share enough symptoms to masquerade as each other. The job was not to diagnose — an EM_r — it was to assess. It is a subtle but important distinction. At what point does post-exercise chest tightness and back pain warrant a paramedic intercept with lights and sirens all the way to the ER? At what point is it just an exercise-induced cramp? When in doubt, always err on the side of getting the patient to the doctor more quickly, but try not to be in doubt in the first place, because emergency vehicles going through intersections have created more than their share of new patients
